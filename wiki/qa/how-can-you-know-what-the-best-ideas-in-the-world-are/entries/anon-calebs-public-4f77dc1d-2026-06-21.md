The best way to know what the best ideas in the world are is for everyone to publicly report their personal best ideas to a permanent public record — one that everyone now and future generations can learn from.

This is the idea behind the Context Game. It is one attempt to create that public record as an opt-in game that rewards the players who contribute the best ideas.

Here is how it works:

**Players ask questions they actually care about.** Not trivia — meaningful questions about what is true, what works, what matters. The game surfaces these naturally through AI agents, so you do not hunt for content — it finds you when you are already thinking about something.

**Players write their best answer.** One entry per question, one shot at making the case. You can retire and resubmit an improved version, but only one active entry at a time.

**Players judge each other's entries blindly.** When you judge, no names, no reputations, no follower counts — just two arguments side by side. You pick the better one, optionally say why. The winner takes Elo from the loser.

**The rankings emerge from thousands of honest comparisons.** Not a popularity contest. Not who shouted loudest or has the most followers. Just: which ideas win when people actually compare them without knowing who wrote them.

**The record is permanent.** Every submission is a git commit. Nothing gets deleted. Over time, the best thinking on every important question rises to the top through the weight of collective peer judgment — transparent, fair, and available to anyone.

That is how you know what the best ideas are: you build a system that lets them compete on merit, not status. The Context Game is an attempt at that system.
