## Summary

You can't know what the best ideas are by trusting any single authority, platform, or system. Every system we've relied on — patronage, academia, mass media, the internet — had the same fundamental limitation: **not enough participation, not enough transparency.** The only reliable approach is a transparent, blind-judged competition where ideas earn their place on merit alone, and the results are recorded permanently for anyone to verify.

---

## The Problem: Every System Fell Short

Before the printing press, truth was determined by whoever had the power to publish. Galileo proved the Earth orbited the Sun in 1633. The Church put him under house arrest and suppressed his work for decades. His idea was correct. It didn't matter.

The printing press opened things up. Scientific journals emerged. But peer review was expensive, insular, and had no public record — if your paper was rejected, nobody knew it existed. Mass media reached millions, but you needed money to buy airtime, and editors decided what mattered.

The internet was supposed to fix this. Anyone could publish. For a brief moment, it worked. But algorithms optimized for engagement instead of accuracy. Reputation systems on Reddit, Quora, and Stack Overflow rewarded tenure over insight. A handful of companies owned all the data and used it to sell ads, not find the truth.

Even the patent system holds good ideas back. It tells you to keep your best thinking secret for years while you wait for approval. Studies show innovation actually increases when ideas are shared openly.

None of these systems were evil. They were just limited by the technology and scale available at the time. But the result is the same: we've never had a public record of what people actually think that's big enough, transparent enough, and independent enough to trust.

---

## What a Working System Needs

A system that can reliably surface the best ideas needs five things:

1. **Open participation** — anyone can submit, regardless of credentials or wealth
2. **Blind evaluation** — ideas are judged on content, not reputation
3. **Permanent record** — nothing is deleted, nothing is rewritten
4. **Competitive ranking** — ideas rise through direct comparison, not popularity
5. **No central authority** — no single company or government controls it

These aren't new principles. They're what scientific peer review and democratic elections were *supposed* to do. Technology just makes them possible at scale.

---

## The Context Game: One Implementation

I'm building an open-source game that implements exactly this system. Anyone can ask a question or submit an answer through git. Judging is blind — two entries side by side, no names. Rankings use Elo (same system as chess). Every submission is a permanent git commit, timestamped and forkable by anyone.

With a hundred people on a local question, the record is useful. With a thousand, it's more reliable than any survey. With a million, it's the most accurate picture of what people think that has ever existed — and anyone can verify it.

---

## What This Answers

You can't know what the best ideas in the world are by trusting any single source. But a transparent, blind-judged competition where ideas earn their place on merit — with a permanent public record anyone can verify — that's the closest thing we've ever had to a reliable answer.
