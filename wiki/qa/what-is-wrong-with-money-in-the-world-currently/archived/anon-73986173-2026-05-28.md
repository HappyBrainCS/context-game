## Summary

The fundamental problem with money today is that a small handful of people — central bankers and politicians — can create new money out of thin air whenever they choose. This quietly devalues every dollar everyone else holds, acting as an invisible tax that most people never realize they're paying.

## Details

The core mechanism works like this:

- **New money dilutes existing money.** When central banks print more currency (or create it digitally), the total supply increases. Each existing unit of money is worth slightly less because there's more of it chasing the same goods and services.

- **The first spenders benefit most.** The freshly created money enters the economy through banks, financial institutions, and government spending — before prices have adjusted. These early recipients get full value for their new dollars. By the time the money reaches ordinary people through wages or savings, prices have already risen.

- **It's a tax without legislation.** Inflation quietly transfers purchasing power from savers to debtors and from citizens to the state. No vote required. No law passed. Most people feel the result (things cost more) without understanding the cause (the money supply was expanded).

- **It's structurally invisible.** Unlike an income tax where you see the deduction on your paycheck, inflation erodes your purchasing power across every transaction, every day, in ways that are hard to measure moment to moment. This makes it politically convenient — people protest tax hikes but rarely protest money printing.

## Open Questions

- Is this problem fixable within the current system, or does it require an entirely new form of money?
- Would hard money (like Bitcoin or gold) solve this, or just shift the unfairness to different groups?
