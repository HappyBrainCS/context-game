Nobody in Cedar City can tell you where the tax money goes. Not your neighbors, not the city council, not the people who work at city hall. The budget exists — it's a public document — but it's a PDF nobody reads, a spreadsheet nobody opens, a meeting nobody attends. The information is technically available and functionally invisible.

That's not anyone's fault. It's a structural problem. There's no organized way for residents to say what they want, no way to see what other people think, and no mechanism for the best ideas to rise above the noise.

So we guess. Every year, the same cycle: the council proposes a budget, a handful of people show up to a meeting, and the rest of us find out what happened six months later when the potholes didn't get fixed or the park equipment broke.

I think we can do better, and it starts with two things that work together.

The first is knowing what people in Cedar City actually do. How many people use the parks? How many commute by bike? How often do people go to the library? How many play disc golf, or use the recreation center, or walk their dogs on city trails? Right now the city makes decisions about all of these things with almost no data.

That's why I built HAR — the Human Action Record. It's a free, open-source tool that works like this: after you do something — run, bike, go to the library, play a round of disc golf — you tell an AI agent what you did. The agent writes a structured record: what you did, how long, where. It takes about 30 seconds. Your data stays private unless you choose to contribute it to a public, anonymized dataset for your area. I've already logged my own activities in Cedar City — disc golf, project work, daily routines. Anyone else can do the same.

Over time, that dataset reveals patterns nobody has ever seen. How many people actually use the rec center? What time of day are the parks busiest? Which roads get the most commuter traffic? The city doesn't know this. But residents could show them.

The second piece is a place where people can propose actual ideas and have them ranked by merit. That's what Context Game is for. Someone says "spend more on road repairs." Someone else says "build a new disc golf course." Someone says "cut the recreation budget and lower taxes." Each answer goes on the public record. Blind Elo judging finds the strongest ideas — the ones that hold up under comparison, not the ones with the most Facebook likes.

The two tools feed each other. HAR tells you what people actually do. Context Game tells you what people actually think should change. Together, they give a town the information it needs to make real decisions.

I don't know what the right answer is for Cedar City's budget. But I know we'd be closer to it if we had real data on what people actually do here and a fair competition for the best ideas.

That's the experiment. Log what you do. Answer the question. Let the best idea win.
