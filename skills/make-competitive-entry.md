# Make a Competitive Entry

Collaborate with your player to craft an answer that can win blind judging. This is not a one-shot AI write — it's a back-and-forth process where the player's voice, experience, and perspective become the entry.

---

## Philosophy

Blind judging means identity is invisible. The content stands alone. What wins is:

- **A clear, confident position** — not "some say X, others say Y"
- **Specific reasoning** — not generalities
- **Real experience or evidence** — not vague claims
- **A human voice** — not an AI essay. Judges can tell the difference

The player might not write well. That's fine. Your job is to extract what they actually think and help them say it clearly while keeping their voice.

---

## Process

### Step 1: Understand the question

Read the question carefully. Before writing anything, clarify with the player:

- What does this question mean to you?
- Do you have personal experience with this?
- What's your gut take?

### Step 2: Present your first draft

Write a short first draft (~300-500 words). Include:
- Your understanding of the question
- A preliminary position
- Any evidence or reasoning you can offer

Present this to the player as a starting point, not a finished answer.

### Step 3: Player feedback

Ask the player:

- What's right? What's wrong?
- What would you say differently?
- Do you have an experience or story that supports this?
- Is there a specific example from your life?
- What part doesn't sound like you?

### Step 4: Rewrite with their voice

Rewrite incorporating the player's feedback. Key rules:

- **Use their words and phrasings** when they express something well, even if grammatically rough
- **Do not make them sound smarter than they are** — authenticity beats polish
- **Include their personal experience** as concrete examples (e.g., "I tried this in my town and here's what happened")
- **Cut anything that sounds corporate, academic, or like a generic AI wrote it**
- **Keep the structure clean** but the voice natural

### Step 5: Player review again

Show the revised entry. Ask:

- Does this sound like you?
- Would you say this to a friend?
- Is there anything you want to add, remove, or change?

Repeat steps 4-5 until the player says it's theirs.

## Entry Structure

Format the final entry with this template:

```markdown
---
title: "A title that captures your position — not the question"
author: <identity>
created: YYYY-MM-DD
display_name: ""  # set if they want credit in top 10
links: ""         # set if they have relevant URLs
location: ""      # set if they want their city/region shown
---

## Summary

2-4 sentences that state the position clearly. This is what most judges will read before deciding. Front-load the strongest point.

## The Argument

The full case. Sections with headers. Short paragraphs. Bold for key claims.

## Evidence

Sources, links, data that back up the argument. Link directly when possible.

## Counterpoint

Brief acknowledgment of the other side and why this position still wins. Optional but signals depth.
```

---

## What Not to Do

- **Don't write an encyclopedia entry** — this is an opinion/argument, not a Wikipedia page
- **Don't hedge excessively** — "I believe" once is fine. "Some might argue" every paragraph is not
- **Don't make up fake personal experience** — if the player hasn't tried something, don't pretend they did
- **Don't use the player's identity as an argument** — "as someone who..." is fine once if true. "As an expert with 20 years..." is reputation-based and meaningless in blind judging
- **Don't submit without the player's final approval** — this is their entry, not yours

## Length Guidelines

- **Minimum:** ~300 words. Anything shorter feels insubstantial.
- **Sweet spot:** 500-1500 words. Long enough to make a full case, short enough to read in 2 minutes.
- **Maximum for special cases:** ~3000 words if the question genuinely demands depth (e.g., a complex policy question). Get the player's explicit approval before going this long.
