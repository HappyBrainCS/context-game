# Make a Competitive Entry

Collaborate with your player to craft an answer that can win blind judging. This is not a one-shot AI write — it's a structured process drawing on proven frameworks for argument, explanation, and persuasion.

---

## Philosophy

Blind judging means identity is invisible. The content stands alone. The entry must earn victory through:

- **Clarity** — can a judge grasp the position in 10 seconds?
- **Depth** — does it show real understanding, not just surface-level opinion?
- **Evidence** — are claims backed by sources, examples, or reasoning?
- **Voice** — does it sound like a real person with genuine perspective?
- **Structure** — is it organized so a judge can navigate it easily?

The best entries feel like a well-written article or guide, not a chat response.

---

## Answer Frameworks (Choose One)

Different questions call for different structures. Assess the question and pick the right framework.

### Framework A: The Argument (for debatable questions)

Based on the Toulmin model of argumentation. Best for "should X?" or "what's better?" questions.

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | Your position stated clearly in 1-2 sentences |
| **Grounds** | Evidence, data, facts that support the claim |
| **Warrant** | Why the evidence supports the claim (the logical bridge) |
| **Backing** | Additional support — sources, expert consensus, case studies |
| **Rebuttal** | Acknowledge the strongest counterargument and explain why your position still wins |
| **Qualifier** | Any limits on your claim (when it might not apply) |

### Framework B: The Explanation (for "how/what is?" questions)

Based on the Feynman Technique. Best for explaining a concept clearly.

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Plain-language summary** | Explain it so anyone can understand — no jargon |
| **The core idea** | What it is, how it works, why it matters |
| **Concrete example** | A real or relatable case that illustrates the idea |
| **Deeper dive** | More detail for readers who want it (with sections) |
| **Why it matters now** | Context — why this is relevant today |

### Framework C: The Definitive Guide (for complex questions)

Combines history, analysis, and argument. Best for heavyweight questions like "How can you know..." or "What's actually working in..."

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Executive summary** | The bottom line in 3-4 sentences |
| **History / Context** | How we got here — relevant background |
| **The core argument** | Your position, built progressively |
| **Evidence & examples** | Sources, case studies, historical precedents |
| **Counterarguments** | Address the best objections |
| **Conclusion** | What this means and what should happen next |
| **Timeline / Appendix** | Optional — for questions with a chronological dimension |

### Framework D: The Case Study (for experience-based questions)

Best when the player has personal experience that makes the answer unique.

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **The question** | Restate what you're answering |
| **My experience** | What happened, what I tried, what I learned |
| **What worked** | The specific approach that succeeded |
| **What didn't** | Failures and dead ends — often more valuable |
| **Advice** | What I'd tell someone else in the same position |
| **Sources** | Links, data, references that back up the experience |

## Process

### Step 1: Assess the question

Before drafting, determine:
- **Type:** Is this debatable (Framework A), explanatory (B), complex (C), or experiential (D)?
- **Depth needed:** A quick take (500 words) or a thorough treatment (1000-2000 words)?
- **Player's position:** What does the player actually think? Get their gut take first.

### Step 2: Research

Before writing, gather:
- Relevant history or context (e.g., Galileo for censorship)
- Supporting data or sources
- Strong counterarguments to anticipate
- Examples that illustrate the point

Do not make claims without sources if sources are available. A judge comparing two entries will feel the difference between "studies show" and a linked citation.

### Step 3: Present a structured first draft

Write a draft using the chosen framework. Include:
- A strong title that captures the position, not just restates the question
- Clear section headers
- Inline sources where applicable
- A counterargument section (even if brief — it signals depth)

Present this to the player as a starting point, not a finished answer.

### Step 4: Player feedback

Ask the player:
- What's right? What's wrong?
- What would you say differently?
- Do you have personal experience that belongs here?
- What part doesn't sound like you?
- Is the structure working?

### Step 5: Rewrite with their voice

Incorporate feedback. Key rules:
- **Use their words and phrasings** when they express something well
- **Do not overwrite** — authenticity beats polish
- **Include their personal experience** as concrete examples
- **Cut anything that sounds like generic AI writing**
- **Keep the structure clean** but the voice natural

### Step 6: Final review

Show the revised entry. Ask:
- Does this sound like you?
- Is the argument clear if you read only the summary?
- Is there anything you want to add, remove, or change?
- Would you want to read this if someone else wrote it?

Repeat steps 4-5 until the player says it's theirs.

## Entry Format

```markdown
---
title: "Title that captures your position"
author: <identity>
created: YYYY-MM-DD
display_name: ""  # set if they want credit in top 10
links: ""         # relevant URLs
location: ""      # city/region if relevant
---

## Summary

3-4 sentences. This is what most judges read before deciding. Front-load the strongest point. State the position clearly.

## (Section header from chosen framework)

Content with headers, short paragraphs, bold for key claims.

## Sources

Links, citations, references that back up the argument. Inline links preferred where possible.
```

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting, verify:
- [ ] Clear position stated in the first section
- [ ] Every claim has backing (source, example, or reasoning)
- [ ] Counterargument acknowledged (even briefly)
- [ ] Human voice — reads like a person, not an LLM
- [ ] Structure is scannable (headers, bold, short paragraphs)
- [ ] Title captures the position, not the question
- [ ] No identity signaling (no "as an expert," no name-dropping)
- [ ] Player has approved the final version

## Length Guidelines

**Default to short and scannable.** The best entries read like a great article you'd share — not a dissertation. Aim to make your point, back it up, and get out.

| Depth | Length | When |
|---|---|---|
| Quick take (default) | 300-600 words | Most questions, first entries. Tight, scannable, shareable |
| Standard | 600-1000 words | Questions needing examples or modest depth |
| Thorough | 1000-1500 words | Complex questions requiring history/evidence |
| Reference | 1500+ words | Only with explicit player approval for exceptional depth |

## What Not to Do

- Don't write an encyclopedia entry — this is an argument/opinion, not a Wikipedia page
- Don't hedge excessively — state the position confidently
- Don't invent fake personal experience
- Don't use identity as an argument — blind judging means content-only
- Don't submit without final player approval
- Don't default to long — shorter is harder and better. If you can say it in 400 words, don't write 800
